Serra and the Hedgehog

Created: May 10, 2026

There is a sculpture behind Peyton Hall. Three large sheets of steel, rusty, about twice my height, standing more or less parallel and curving slightly, creating a narrow passage in between. There may have been a sign saying do not enter, but during my years at Peyton I’ve trespassed the statue numerous times, partly because it was a shortcut, saving a step or two on my way to the train station, and largely because it was a fun walk; for a moment, the rusty steel surrounds you, with entrance and exit both hidden by the curve.

The name of the sculpture is The Hedgehog and the Fox. I learned it on one of my first passages through it, as excessive paper-reading and museum-going had left me with the habit of searching for a caption for everything. Apparently it came from some Greek poet saying that the fox knows many things but the hedgehog knows one great thing. I don’t know what the canonical reading is. At first I thought it was praising the hedgehog for its focus - I have a biased view of the fox when it appears in a Greek parable context - but I changed my mind after seeing the foxes and hedgehogs around me and decided that I liked neither. Eventually I had too much in life and astrophysics to worry about and gave up on reading into the reference or contemplating why three large sheets of rusty steel should be given the names of Greek parable animals. (I have since learned a few interpretations, including the canonical explanation from the artist himself; they aren’t bad, but I prefer not to deprive you of the fun of the uncertainty.)

The artist who made the sculpture is Richard Serra. I must have seen the name on the plate next to the sculpture, during my search for the hedgehog’s origin, and between the emails where one senior member of the department shared the anecdote of a dispute around the sculpture decades ago - with a complaint from either the department or the university or the artist - during the bleak days of the pandemic, along with emails of how another senior member of the department tested Peyton Hall’s ventilation system by placing some perfume in the student lounge and checking if he could smell it elsewhere.

But the name Serra did not register until much later. I, with my usual academic cynicism, preferred to remember works, not names. The dots were connected years later, when I began to notice his work in museums. The style is not hard to recognize; after all, he made several other pieces that are nearly identical, but with easier-to-understand names like Snake and Double Torqued Ellipse. And by now I can often instinctively spot a Serra among other grotesque abstractions in Museums of Modern Art with Various Prefixes and Suffixes.

My latest Serra spotting happened today, when I took an afternoon walk to SFMoMA. It was the time between two major exhibitions (the one just finished wasted half a floor for six months and made me question my decision to pay for membership), and thanks to that the floors were emptier than usual, of both art and humans. After my last visit, they had rearranged the permanent display, and Serra now had a corner of his own with a rusty steel box, two less rusty steel pieces leaning against the wall, a sculpture of steel pieces taking the form of a painting, and a short introduction. The rusty steel triggered my nostalgia (together with the slight trauma of leaving academia and moving to the other side of the country). The introduction got me thinking more about art and Serra himself.

During Serra’s childhood, his father worked as a pipe fitter for a shipyard. Memories of the shipyard ultimately became the rusty steel sheets I have walked through. The stories of many modern artists (perhaps modern American artists in particular) are similar; they drew a lot of inspiration from the world around them, be it steel from ships, Ben-Day dots from comic books, or product ads. It must therefore have felt quite different to be looking at these works around the time they were made, when the pieces were less foreign and more like a parody of what was already present in the audience’s life. And what would a contemporary version of that be, if a contemporary artist took the same strategy? At first I thought it would be something like social media or short videos or AI slop. But thinking more about it, it no longer seems easy to create interesting work this way. Printing things at 5x their real size? Seems pretty unsurprising by now. Putting something that would be very out of place within a TikTok frame? But is there even anything that’s “very out of place” for TikTok? The nature of the sensorial and cultural world around us has changed so much. We no longer have a few common touchstones that support a shared memory - everything is so diverse and overwhelming. Maybe that’s why contemporary works have moved away from this approach.

Another common theme of Serra and other artists of his time was how focused they were. You can doodle lines on your notebook when listening to a talk (at Peyton Hall, I saw my advisor doing that sometimes) and it does not make you an artist. But if you stay meticulous, work in large format, and produce a roomful of paintings of squares and stripes that invoke the fabric selection of an online dress shirt store, you can be Agnes Martin. And I say that with respect and appreciation of Martin and the like. It’s understandable that you want to devote a lot of attention (or soul, if we prefer the more spiritual framing) to a subject to get a nuanced feeling that’s worth sharing. But from the perspective of career and community I can’t help but find it so unfair and restrictive. It takes a lot to be a hedgehog, and not everyone can afford it. What happens to the people who work 8 hours a day and don’t get to go to art schools? How can their voices be heard? Even for the artists themselves, it feels like having a personal brand makes it extra hard to pivot away from the one thing you are great at. But my complaints make little sense - I have very little knowledge of how the art community works, and these are really just me projecting academia onto artists. That is why, despite how much I like art, I haven’t thought about becoming an artist myself; that is why, despite how much I like academic research, I ended up deciding that the community is not for me.

Walking away from Serra’s corner, across a few rooms, and into the sculpture garden on the terrace, I found it all extremely alien. The sight of the buildings around me - the ones I stare at on my way to work when I wait for the traffic light - reminded me that I was still in this city where tokens and dollars float in the air and the smell of animal and human waste ambushes people on the ground. Meanwhile the artworks sit in their white box rooms; a painter (not the artsy kind, but the wall-painting kind) stands at the end of the hallway, but with no smell of paint, and turns out the painter is another statue. The sculpture garden is this in-between state where you get the city skyline but not the pee smell, and the sculptures (maybe another Serra was on that terrace, I can’t remember) but not the quiet white box. The amalgamation forced me to admit that the museum is not a portal to a different dimension, and that caused me some discomfort. It was then that I realized I had been using museums as a temporary escape from this world. Maybe that’s also why I wanted to walk through The Hedgehog and the Fox and enjoy a moment of rusty steel screening away the campus, leaving only a glimpse of the sky, somehow always clear in my memory. The rust, according to the internet, is enhanced on purpose to make a foxy red. To my eye it’s still far from a fox’s color, and I also wonder where this leaves the hedgehog, which the sculpture bears zero resemblance to. Maybe Serra just wanted me to play the hedgehog.
