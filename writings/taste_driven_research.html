What Happened to Research Taste?

Created: February 14, 2026

Something has changed in how research works—or at least in how it feels.

I’ve heard versions of the same unease from theorists across fields. The sense that “good taste”—knowing what’s worth working on, which directions are promising—matters less than it used to. That there’s more randomness in what succeeds. That research feels less like navigation and more like a lottery, or following a herd.

Similar frustrations show up online: complaints about “publish or perish” pressures favoring safe, incremental work over ambitious research; discussions of troubling trends in ML scholarship where researchers chase benchmarks and bandwagons; laments that choosing good problems is rarely taught. The grievances vary, but circle a common theme: something about how research directions get chosen feels off.

This article tries to give that feeling shape—a frame for what might have shifted, why, and what it might mean.

Taste-driven vs. chance-driven research

Consider two modes of research, idealized as opposite poles.

In taste-driven research, you develop a sense of what’s missing, what’s ripe, what’s likely to be fruitful. You follow that signal. You have some model of the field—where the gaps are, where promising peaks might be—and navigate toward them. Progress feels like climbing a gradient.

In chance-driven research, you pick a direction that’s tractable—often based on what methods you know, what tools are available, what’s doable—and work on it. Sometimes it leads somewhere interesting, sometimes not. Progress feels like random walk: explore, and occasionally stumble onto something good.

These are poles on a spectrum. No one does purely taste-driven or purely chance-driven research. But the balance can shift—for an individual over a career, for a field over time, for science as a whole. And the subjective sense of many researchers is that we’ve been shifting away from taste-driven and toward chance-driven.

Is this shift real?

How much of this is perception, and how much is real change?

I don’t know for sure. One source of ambiguity is that we talk about research in a way that systematically favors the taste-driven story. Read a paper’s introduction and you’ll often find motivated investigation—“we identified this gap,” “this question remained open,” “we hypothesized that.” Researchers describe their work as guided by what should be done, even when it was in fact driven by what methods happened to be available. This preference shapes how we remember past research too, making it hard to know whether things were ever different.

There’s also a gap between how science is taught and practiced. The “scientific method” most of us learned—hypothesis first, then experiment, then conclusion—presents research as fundamentally taste-driven. You identify a question, form a prediction, test it. The frame assumes the hard part is knowing what to ask. But whether this ever described how research actually worked is unclear. It may always have been more ideal than reality. (Kuhn made this point decades ago—textbook science systematically distorts how discovery happens.)

So part of the frustration may be this: the way we’re taught to think about research doesn’t match how it works, and perhaps never did. That dissonance is something many feel, even without naming it.

But I suspect there’s also a real shift—that taste-driven research is genuinely losing ground, not just in perception. The next section offers a hypothesis for why.

Why taste-driven research might be losing ground

My hypothesis: this is less about researchers choosing differently and more about what the market selects for. Chance-driven research has become more economical, and the structural reasons have been accumulating.

Growing burden of knowledge. As fields mature, developing genuine taste—a calibrated sense of what’s important and tractable—requires knowing more. The landscape gets larger, more complex. Harder for anyone to see clearly enough to navigate well. Meanwhile, random walk stays easy: learn what your advisor does, make a random variation, see what happens. (This is closer to evolutionary algorithm than pure random walk, but both share the same spirit—you don’t need the bigger picture.)

Expanding researcher population. More researchers means more parallel exploration. If each does something like random walk, more walkers cover more ground. Wider search becomes more efficient than many people working the same “correct” direction—especially when no one’s confident what correct is.

Faster communication and feedback loop. Results propagate quickly. Promising directions get noticed and amplified. The selection loop tightens: try things, publish, see what gets traction, follow the traction. Fast iteration beats slow deliberation.

The field becomes the Bayesian agent. Individual researchers explore semi-arbitrarily. But the publication-citation-funding system filters—amplifying what works, starving what doesn’t. The taste function has migrated from individuals to collective mechanism. The field does Bayesian search, even if no individual does.

This process is self-reinforcing. As collective search improves, individual taste becomes harder to compete with. You can still develop good judgment through deep study—but by the time you develop that judgment, the frontier has moved. You’re perpetually catching up. The lag between understanding and frontier makes taste-driven work increasingly uneconomical.

One consequence I’d like to highlight: the typical mode of theoretical work moves from prediction and guidance before new findings to explanation and synthesis after them. The theorist becomes less the navigator and more the mapmaker who arrives after the territory has already been explored.

Is this a bad thing?

It depends on what you’re asking about.

On the objective side:

For the field’s progress, the shift is probably fine—maybe even beneficial. Collective search may be more efficient than individual taste, especially given how hard it’s become to see the whole landscape. Understanding still gets built, just in different order. The old pattern: theory predicts, experiment confirms. The new pattern: experiment discovers, theory explains, further experiment refines. The endpoint is similar; the path is different.

Structurally, there may be a real problem, but not the one you’d first expect. The issue isn’t that chance-driven exploration produces less understanding. It’s that later steps, consolidation and theorizing after discovery, may be undersupported. Funding and attention concentrate on the frontier, the next result. Making sense of what’s already found—building maps after territory is explored—gets less reward. There’s a related asymmetry: forming explanatory theories is cheap, but testing them is expensive. In the old theory→verification path, you were forced to put forward predictions that could be checked; wrong theories got weeded out. But explanatory theories that arrive after the data can fit the facts without being truly predictive—and wrong theories that still appear to explain something get cited anyway. The shift in timing creates a quality-control problem.

On the subjective side:

For some researchers, yes—something is lost. Some find deep satisfaction in identifying good directions: the feeling that judgment matters, that you’re navigating not wandering, that thinking quality shapes outcome quality. When the system shifts toward chance-driven search, this satisfaction becomes harder to find. Not because you’re doing anything wrong, but because what you valued is less rewarded. The connection between taste and outcome weakens. Research feels more like lottery, and some don’t like lotteries.

This cuts both ways. Researchers who enjoy stumbling onto something unanticipated might thrive. Surprise becomes more central. And experimentalists may finally get recognition proportional to contribution. The cultural narrative has emphasized theoretical insight; the Physics Nobel has actually gone roughly 2:1 to experimentalists, but you wouldn’t know from how we talk about great science. The current shift might correct an old imbalance.

Finding a way out

If you value taste-driven work in this increasingly chance-driven landscape, what are the options? Here are a few directions I can imagine.

Go with the current. Embrace exploration. Shift satisfaction from “I identified a good direction” to “I explored quickly and stumbled onto something interesting.” Learn to enjoy surprises. Accept that outcomes are loosely coupled to judgment, and find a way to be okay with that. This is probably the most practical option for most people. It asks you to change what you want, which is hard, but it aligns you with the current.

Try to scale taste. If chance-driven research wins because it parallelizes, maybe taste-driven research can also become more competitive. The bottleneck for taste-driven work is integration—building a picture coherent enough to identify promising directions before exploring them. This has traditionally been slow, limited by one person’s bandwidth and memory, unable to keep pace with the frontier. But AI might change that. LLMs are good at gathering and sifting information; paired with a researcher’s taste and judgment, they could accelerate integration substantially. More generally, increased connectivity might also enable better ways to articulate and transmit taste and heuristics, or form collaborative taste where small groups with complementary knowledge identify directions none would see alone. The caveat is that I don’t know whether any of this is enough to catch up.

Treat it as a hobby. Do taste-driven work for intrinsic satisfaction, without expecting it to be competitive. Let the field’s efficiency be carried by others. Enjoy identifying good directions, building coherent pictures, even if not the most efficient path. Many valuable activities have become hobbies as they’ve been industrialized—woodworking, painting, amateur astronomy. Taste-driven research could join that list.

The landscape could change again

The current situation isn’t permanent. In particular, AI might soon shift the balance unpredictably. AI scales strictly better than humans at exploration—equally parallelizable, and compute grows faster than researcher population. If AI takes over most random walk, humans might find comparative advantage shifting toward taste—judgment calls, synthesis, “what does this mean” questions. Gradient estimation becomes the human contribution, though perhaps a small fraction of the whole. Alternatively, AI could get good at integration too, and we’ll be forced to accept that human research is just a hobby.

What would it mean to do taste-driven research well in this landscape? I’m not sure. The answer probably looks different from what most of us were taught.

Regardless, there’s something to be said for doing careful, taste-driven work even when the world doesn’t reward it, just like there’s something to be said for woodworking in a world of mass-produced furniture. The meaning is in the practice itself, not in whether it’s economically competitive. Maybe that’s enough. Maybe it always was.
